Red Flag #1
The reviewer has only 1-2 reviews ever
Real people review lots of places over time. If someone's only review is a glowing 5-star essay for one business? That's a burner account. Fake review farms create throwaway profiles that post once and disappear.
What it looks like: "John S." — 1 review, 0 photos, joined last month. Wrote 3 paragraphs about how a dentist "changed their life."
Red Flag #2
A bunch of reviews posted on the same day
If a business suddenly gets 10 five-star reviews in 48 hours? They probably bought a package. Real reviews trickle in over weeks and months — they don't show up like a flash mob.
What it looks like: A coffee shop with 3 reviews per month suddenly gets 15 five-star reviews in one weekend. All of them say "best coffee in town!"
Red Flag #3
The language is weirdly generic
Fake reviewers can't describe real experiences because they never had them. They write vague, copy-paste compliments that could apply to literally any business. AI-generated reviews grew 80% month-over-month since 2023 — and they all sound the same.
What it looks like: "Great food, great service, great atmosphere. Will definitely come back. Highly recommend!" — That could be about a taco truck or a Michelin-star restaurant. Zero specifics = zero trust.
Red Flag #4
They mention things that don't exist
This is the dead giveaway. Fake reviewers (and AI bots) sometimes reference menu items, features, or details that the business doesn't even have. They're working from a script, not from experience.
What it looks like: "Loved the rooftop patio!" — the restaurant doesn't have a patio. "The lobster bisque was amazing!" — it's a pizza place. If the review doesn't match reality, it's fake.
Red Flag #5
Suspicious timing after bad press
Business gets a viral 1-star TikTok? Health inspection fails? Bad news article? Then — like magic — a flood of 5-star reviews appears within days. That's damage control, not happy customers. The FTC can now fine businesses $53,000 per fake review for this.
What it looks like: A restaurant gets called out on social media on Monday. By Friday, 20 new 5-star reviews appear, all with the same "hidden gem" language. The dates don't lie.

Quick Tips to Protect Yourself

  • Sort by "newest" instead of "most relevant" — fake reviews get buried in old results
  • Click on the reviewer's profile. Real people have dozens of reviews across different businesses
  • Look at 3-star reviews. Fakers almost never write 3-star reviews — they go 5 or 1
  • Check for photos. Real customers take real (usually bad) photos. Fake reviewers rarely do
  • Use sites like Fakespot or ReviewMeta to scan Amazon listings instantly
  • If ALL the reviews sound the same, they probably came from the same person (or bot)
